# Personas: eVcN

*eVcN is a two-sided product, so it has two data-driven personas: the **primary** rider (demand side) and the **secondary** station owner (supply side). Designing for both keeps the flywheel — more available chargers for riders ⇄ more revenue for owners — front of mind.*

---

# Primary Persona: "Commuter Linh" (the Rider)

## Descriptive
*   **Narrative:** Linh is a 27-year-old marketing executive who recently traded her petrol Honda for a VinFast Feliz S to save money and avoid the rising cost (and smell) of fuel. She lives in a Binh Thanh apartment where the building won't allow her to charge the bike overnight, and she commutes ~8 km into District 1 every day. She loves the bike — but twice a week she has a small panic about where and when she'll top up.
*   **Demographic:** 27, Ho Chi Minh City, office worker, middle income, smartphone-first.
*   **Psychographic:** Practical and a little time-poor. Digitally native — pays with MoMo/ZaloPay, lives in Zalo and Google Maps, makes fast decisions. Values reliability and "not looking stupid" (arriving somewhere with no free charger). Mildly green-conscious but mostly cost- and convenience-driven.
*   **Maps to workshop segment:** mostly an **Active Explorer** (will describe a vague need and wants help resolving it) with a streak of **Passive Consumer** ("just tell me the best one, don't make me compare five tabs").

## Motivations
*   **Long-term:** a stress-free, cheaper-than-petrol daily commute; never being stranded.
*   **Short-term:** get to ~80% before the evening ride home without a detour or a wait.
*   **Core desires:** **Convenience** (one tap), **Security** (a guaranteed free port), and **Confidence** (knowing the cost and time *before* committing).

## Interactions
*   **Devices/channels:** mobile-only; quick sessions of 30–90 seconds; comfortable with chat-style and map interfaces.
*   **Frequency:** charges 3–5× per week, so she touches the charging problem far more often than a car owner would.
*   **Current habits:** checks battery %, mentally lists 2–3 stations she "thinks" are nearby, and hopes one is free. Uses Google Maps for directions but it doesn't tell her if a charger is *available* or *fast enough*.

## Pain Points
*   **Can't charge at home** (apartment fire-safety restrictions) → fully dependent on public chargers.
*   **Range anxiety** on a small ~4 kWh battery.
*   **No live availability** — she can't tell if ports are free, the wait is long, or the charger is faulty until she arrives (e.g., Binh Thanh Metro shows 0/3 ports, 18-min wait).
*   **Comparison overload** — distance vs. price (3,300–5,200 VND/kWh) vs. speed (Standard/Fast/Ultra-fast) vs. wait, with no single answer.
*   **Wasted trips** to closed stations (e.g., Tan Binh is closed in the network).

## Moments that Matter
*   The moment her battery drops to ~20% mid-commute and she has to decide *now*.
*   The first time she asks the Copilot a messy, real-world question ("charge near District 1 before 6pm") — does it actually understand and give *one* good answer?
*   Arriving to find the port she expected is taken (trust lost forever) vs. seeing her **reservation** honored (trust won).
*   The booking-confirmation screen — the 5 seconds that decide whether she believes eVcN is reliable.

# Secondary Persona: "Operator Tuan" (the Station Owner)

## Descriptive
*   **Narrative:** Tuan, 45, owns a parking lot and small café in District 7 and installed four chargers as a side income. He's not technical. He wants the chargers busy and working, but he only finds out something's wrong when a customer complains or when the monthly electricity bill doesn't match what he thought he earned.
*   **Demographic:** 45, HCMC small-business owner, moderate tech comfort, manages on his phone between other tasks.
*   **Psychographic:** Pragmatic, ROI-driven, time-starved. Trusts simple numbers over dashboards full of jargon. Wants "what should I do today?" not raw analytics.

## Motivations
*   **Long-term:** turn the charger corner into a dependable, growing revenue line; decide confidently whether to buy more chargers.
*   **Short-term:** keep all ports working during the 5–8pm peak; know today's revenue.
*   **Core desires:** **Uptime**, **Revenue visibility**, and **Simple guidance**.

## Interactions
*   **Devices/channels:** phone, glanced at a few times a day; prefers a single screen with the headline numbers.
*   **Frequency:** checks in the morning and around the evening peak.
*   **Current habits:** walks the lot to see which chargers are in use; tracks revenue loosely or not at all.

## Pain Points
*   **No visibility** into utilization (his District 1-equivalent hub could be at 86–92% and he wouldn't know).
*   **Faults discovered late** — a dead Ultra-fast charger during peak can quietly cost ~18% of a day's revenue.
*   **No sense of peak timing** or whether to add chargers / change pricing.
*   **Fragmented info** — bookings, sessions, and revenue live in different places (or in his head).

## Moments that Matter
*   A charger goes **Faulty** at 6pm on a busy evening — how fast does he find out?
*   End of day: "Did I actually make money today?" (the dashboard's *Revenue today* + *Utilization* answer this).
*   The decision to invest in 2 more fast chargers — he needs evidence, not a gut feeling (the AI insight "District 1 at 86% utilization, consider adding 2 fast chargers" is exactly this moment).
